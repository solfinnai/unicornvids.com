The Bold Bet

They pitched a movie that would never mention the product. Salesforce said yes.

In 2013, our parent company RockBridge Productions had been making Salesforce product demos and customer success stories for years. Solid content, but standard playbook. Then came the pitch that changed everything: a 90-minute feature documentary about the history and art of selling — that would never once utter the word "Salesforce."

The logic was counterintuitive but razor-sharp. One out of every nine people Salesforce engages with works in sales. Instead of pitching them software, what if you gave them something genuinely valuable — a film that honors their profession, teaches their craft, and restores the reputation of an entire industry?

"Our intent was really to take the time to educate people on what the sales profession is and what it takes to be a successful salesperson — hard work and genuine care about customers." — Zaledonis, VP of Product Marketing, Salesforce
The Scale

6 months. 12 locations. 35+ interviews. 60 hours of footage.

This wasn't a weekend shoot. The production spanned six months coast-to-coast across the United States, following a real sales professional through her career journey while weaving in thought leaders, historians, and the untold story of how selling shaped modern commerce.

Over 15 full production crew days and 30+ half-crew days. 60 hours of raw footage, meticulously cut down to an 80-minute runtime. The red-carpet premiere happened in November at Dreamforce — the world's largest tech conference. The only footage not shot by our team were archival clips from the last century.

The Revenue Machine

The film became a content factory that never stopped producing.

The documentary was just the beginning. Salesforce chopped it into blog posts, social clips, email sequences, and gated educational content. Each piece opened conversations with prospects about their professional lives and business challenges — conversations that led directly to pipeline and closed deals.

"We gated some of that additional content, so we were able to use these really inspirational and educational pieces to open even bigger conversations about their professional lives and the business challenges that they face every day. Gating the content generated a significant amount of leads, pipeline, and revenue." — Zaledonis, VP of Product Marketing, Salesforce

On a mid-six-figure production budget, Salesforce generated a 10x return on investment within the first 12 months alone — over $20 million in attributed revenue from the film and its derivative content. But here's what makes this truly extraordinary: that was just year one. The documentary has continued generating leads, pipeline, and revenue every single year since its 2013 launch. Universities teach it. Sales teams screen it for new hires. Thirteen years later, the dedicated page is still live on Salesforce.com. That's not a campaign. That's a compounding asset.

Your Playbook

How your brand can copy this.

Salesforce proved the model. Here's how to apply it at any budget level.

01

Serve your audience, not your ego

The film worked because it gave genuine value to salespeople — not a 90-minute commercial. Create content your audience would seek out even if your logo wasn't on it. Great content promotes itself and people will seek out the source.

02

Target the overlooked audience

Salesforce realized 1 in 9 of their contacts were in sales — not their "main" buyer. By creating something specifically for that group, they opened a massive new pipeline. Look at who's adjacent to your ideal customer.

03

Build a content factory, not a one-shot

One documentary became hundreds of pieces — blog posts, gated assets, social clips, email sequences. Plan your long-form content as a system, not a single deliverable. The derivative content is where the leads live.

04

Be brave enough to not sell

The word "Salesforce" never appeared in the film. That was the point. When you create genuine value, you don't need a hard sell — the trust and authority you build does the selling for you. Any other company would have laughed this off. Salesforce took a calculated risk and it paid off massively.
